# Coarticulation Study: /d/ + /b/

### Sequence: voiced alveolar stop → voiced bilabial stop

### Example Phrase: **bad book**

**IPA:** `/bæd̚ bʊk/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[bæd̚ bʊk[`

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the bilabial target before the /d/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and bilabial articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /d/ and /b/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Stop Release Overlap:**
   The /d/ is typically unreleased ([d̚]), with air pressure maintained until the /b/ closure forms. The release burst belongs to /b/.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Voiced homorganic sequence: voicing maintained throughout

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"bad"** slowly, establishing full /d/ articulation.
2. Say **"book"** starting with an isolated /b/.
3. Merge: *bad book* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/bæd̚ bʊk/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /d/ and /b/.
* Alternate "d-b-d-b" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

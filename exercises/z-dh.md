# Coarticulation Study: /z/ + /ð/

### Sequence: voiced alveolar fricative → voiced dental fricative

### Example Phrase: **has the**

**IPA:** `/hæz̪ ð̪ə/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[hæz̪ ð̪ə[`

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the dental target before the /z/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and dental articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /z/ and /ð/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Voiced alveolar → dental

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"has"** slowly, establishing full /z/ articulation.
2. Say **"the"** starting with an isolated /ð/.
3. Merge: *has the* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/hæz̪ ð̪ə/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /z/ and /ð/.
* Alternate "z-ð-z-ð" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

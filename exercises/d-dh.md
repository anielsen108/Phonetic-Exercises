# Coarticulation Study: /d/ + /ð/

### Sequence: voiced alveolar stop → voiced dental fricative

### Example Phrase: **read the book**

**IPA:** `/ɹid̪̚ ð̪ə bʊk/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[ɹid̪̚ ð̪ə bʊk[`

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the dental target before the /d/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and dental articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /d/ and /ð/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Stop Release Modification:**
   The /d/ closure releases directly into the /ð/ articulation, often showing reduced or absent burst energy.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Dentalized /d̪̚/: tongue tip moves forward to teeth

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"read"** slowly, establishing full /d/ articulation.
2. Say **"the book"** starting with an isolated /ð/.
3. Merge: *read the book* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/ɹid̪̚ ð̪ə bʊk/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /d/ and /ð/.
* Alternate "d-ð-d-ð" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

# Coarticulation Study: /b/ + /r/

### Sequence: voiced bilabial stop → alveolar approximant

### Example Phrase: **cab ride**

**IPA:** `/kʰæb̚ ɹaɪd/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[kʰæb̚ ɹaɪd[`

---

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |

| First | /b/ | bilabial | stop (plosive) | voiced | oral | short closure, often unreleased |

* **/b/**: Bilabial → rhotic
* **/r/**: Alveolar approximant articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the alveolar/postalveolar target before the /b/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between bilabial and alveolar/postalveolar articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /b/ and /r/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Stop Release Modification:**
   The /b/ closure releases directly into the /r/ articulation, often showing reduced or absent burst energy.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Bilabial → rhotic

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"cab"** slowly, establishing full /b/ articulation.
2. Say **"ride"** starting with an isolated /r/.
3. Merge: *cab ride* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/kʰæb̚ ɹaɪd/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /b/ and /r/.
* Alternate "b-r-b-r" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

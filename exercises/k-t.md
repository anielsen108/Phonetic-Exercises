# Coarticulation Study: /k/ + /t/

### Sequence: voiceless velar stop → voiceless alveolar stop

### Example Phrase: **backed**

**IPA:** `/bækt̚/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[bækt̚[`

---

## 1. Segmental Foundations

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|

* **/k/**: Released /k/, unreleased /t̚/
* **/t/**: Voiceless alveolar stop articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the alveolar target before the /k/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between velar and alveolar articulations.

2. **Stop Release Overlap:**
   The /k/ is typically unreleased ([k̚]), with air pressure maintained until the /t/ closure forms. The release burst belongs to /t/.

3. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Released /k/, unreleased /t̚/

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"backed"** slowly, establishing full /k/ articulation.
2. Say **"t"** starting with an isolated /t/.
3. Merge: *backed* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/bækt̚/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /k/ and /t/.
* Alternate "k-t-k-t" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

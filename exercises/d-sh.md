# Coarticulation Study: /d/ + /ʃ/

### Sequence: voiced alveolar stop → voiceless postalveolar fricative

### Example Phrase: **had shoes**

**IPA:** `/hæd̥̚ ʃuz/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[hæd̥̚ ʃuz[`

---

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|

| Second | /ʃ/ | postalveolar | fricative (sibilant) | voiceless | oral | continuous, lower pitch than /s/ |

* **/d/**: Devoicing + slight retraction: [d̥] → [ʃ]
* **/ʃ/**: Voiceless postalveolar fricative articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the postalveolar target before the /d/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and postalveolar articulations.

2. **Voicing Transition:**
   The glottis shifts from voiced to voiceless. This transition may show partial devoicing or voicing anticipation depending on speech rate.

3. **Stop Release Modification:**
   The /d/ closure releases directly into the /ʃ/ articulation, often showing reduced or absent burst energy.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Devoicing + slight retraction: [d̥] → [ʃ]

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"had"** slowly, establishing full /d/ articulation.
2. Say **"shoes"** starting with an isolated /ʃ/.
3. Merge: *had shoes* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/hæd̥̚ ʃuz/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /d/ and /ʃ/.
* Alternate "d-ʃ-d-ʃ" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

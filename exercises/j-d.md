# Coarticulation Study: /j/ + /d/

### Sequence: palatal approximant → voiced alveolar stop

### Example Phrase: **my dog**

**IPA:** `/maɪ dɔɡ/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[maɪ dɔɡ[`

---

## 1. Segmental Foundations

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| First | /j/ | palatal | approximant | voiced | oral | brief glide |
| Second | /d/ | alveolar | stop (plosive) | voiced | oral | variable, flapped intervocalically |

* **/j/**: Palatal → voiced alveolar
* **/d/**: Voiced alveolar stop articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the alveolar target before the /j/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between palatal and alveolar articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /j/ and /d/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Palatal → voiced alveolar

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"my"** slowly, establishing full /j/ articulation.
2. Say **"dog"** starting with an isolated /d/.
3. Merge: *my dog* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/maɪ dɔɡ/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /j/ and /d/.
* Alternate "j-d-j-d" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

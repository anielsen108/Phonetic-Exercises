# Coarticulation Study: /d/ + /j/

### Sequence: voiced alveolar stop → palatal approximant

### Example Phrase: **did you**

**IPA:** `/dɪd͡ʒ ju/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[dɪd͡ʒ ju[`

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the palatal target before the /d/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and palatal articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /d/ and /j/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Stop Release Modification:**
   The /d/ closure releases directly into the /j/ articulation, often showing reduced or absent burst energy.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Coalescence to [d͡ʒ]: palatalization

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"did"** slowly, establishing full /d/ articulation.
2. Say **"you"** starting with an isolated /j/.
3. Merge: *did you* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/dɪd͡ʒ ju/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /d/ and /j/.
* Alternate "d-j-d-j" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

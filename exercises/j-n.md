# Coarticulation Study: /j/ + /n/

### Sequence: palatal approximant → alveolar nasal

### Example Phrase: **my name**

**IPA:** `/maɪ neɪm/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[maɪ neɪm[`

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the alveolar target before the /j/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between palatal and alveolar articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /j/ and /n/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Nasal Coarticulation:**
   Velum movement coordinates with oral articulation. Nasal airflow may extend into or anticipate the adjacent segment.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Palatal → alveolar nasal

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"my"** slowly, establishing full /j/ articulation.
2. Say **"name"** starting with an isolated /n/.
3. Merge: *my name* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/maɪ neɪm/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /j/ and /n/.
* Alternate "j-n-j-n" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

# Coarticulation Study: /n/ + /l/

### Sequence: alveolar nasal → alveolar lateral approximant

### Example Phrase: **in late**

**IPA:** `/ɪn leɪt/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[ɪn leɪt[`

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /n/ and /l/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

2. **Nasal Coarticulation:**
   Velum movement coordinates with oral articulation. Nasal airflow may extend into or anticipate the adjacent segment.

3. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Nasal → lateral

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"in"** slowly, establishing full /n/ articulation.
2. Say **"late"** starting with an isolated /l/.
3. Merge: *in late* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/ɪn leɪt/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /n/ and /l/.
* Alternate "n-l-n-l" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

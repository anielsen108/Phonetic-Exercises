# Coarticulation Study: /f/ + /t/

### Sequence: voiceless labiodental fricative → voiceless alveolar stop

### Example Phrase: **left**

**IPA:** `/lɛft̚/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[lɛft̚[`

---

## 1. Segmental Foundations

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| First | /f/ | labiodental | fricative | voiceless | oral | continuous, variable length |
| Second | /t/ | alveolar | stop (plosive) | voiceless | oral | short, often unreleased word-finally |

* **/f/**: Within-word cluster
* **/t/**: Voiceless alveolar stop articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the alveolar target before the /f/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between labiodental and alveolar articulations.

2. **Fricative-to-Stop Transition:**
   The continuous airflow of /f/ terminates as the /t/ closure forms. Listeners perceive a sharp acoustic boundary.

3. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Within-word cluster

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"left"** slowly, establishing full /f/ articulation.
2. Say **"t"** starting with an isolated /t/.
3. Merge: *left* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/lɛft̚/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /f/ and /t/.
* Alternate "f-t-f-t" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

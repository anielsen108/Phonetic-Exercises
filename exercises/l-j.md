# Coarticulation Study: /l/ + /j/

### Sequence: alveolar lateral approximant → palatal approximant

### Example Phrase: **will you**

**IPA:** `/wɪɫ ju/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[wɪɫ ju[`

---

## 1. Segmental Foundations

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| First | /l/ | alveolar | lateral approximant | voiced | oral (lateral) | continuous, velarized finally |
| Second | /j/ | palatal | approximant | voiced | oral | brief glide |

* **/l/**: Lateral → palatal
* **/j/**: Palatal approximant articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the palatal target before the /l/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and palatal articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /l/ and /j/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Lateral → palatal

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"will"** slowly, establishing full /l/ articulation.
2. Say **"you"** starting with an isolated /j/.
3. Merge: *will you* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/wɪɫ ju/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /l/ and /j/.
* Alternate "l-j-l-j" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the bilabial target before the /ð/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between dental and bilabial articulations.

2. **Voicing Transition:**
   The glottis shifts from voiced to voiceless. This transition may show partial devoicing or voicing anticipation depending on speech rate.

3. **Fricative-to-Stop Transition:**
   The continuous airflow of /ð/ terminates as the /p/ closure forms. Listeners perceive a sharp acoustic boundary.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Devoicing of /ð̥/ before voiceless /p/

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"breathe"** slowly, establishing full /ð/ articulation.
2. Say **"peacefully"** starting with an isolated /p/.
3. Merge: *breathe peacefully* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/bɹið̥̚ ˈpʰisfəli/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /ð/ and /p/.
* Alternate "ð-p-ð-p" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

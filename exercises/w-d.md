# Coarticulation Study: /w/ + /d/

### Sequence: labial-velar approximant → voiced alveolar stop

### Example Phrase: **how did**

**IPA:** `/haʊ dɪd/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[haʊ dɪd[`

---

## 1. Segmental Foundations

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| First | /w/ | labial-velar | approximant | voiced | oral | brief glide |
| Second | /d/ | alveolar | stop (plosive) | voiced | oral | variable, flapped intervocalically |

* **/w/**: Labiovelar → voiced alveolar
* **/d/**: Voiced alveolar stop articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the alveolar target before the /w/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between labial-velar and alveolar articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /w/ and /d/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Labiovelar → voiced alveolar

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"how"** slowly, establishing full /w/ articulation.
2. Say **"did"** starting with an isolated /d/.
3. Merge: *how did* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/haʊ dɪd/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /w/ and /d/.
* Alternate "w-d-w-d" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

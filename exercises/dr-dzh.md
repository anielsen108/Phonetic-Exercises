## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the postalveolar target before the /dr/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and postalveolar articulations.

2. **Voicing Continuity:**
   Both /dr/ and /dʒ/ are voiced; the glottis remains adducted throughout. Spectrographically, this appears as a continuous low-frequency voicing bar.

3. **Stop Release Modification:**
   The /dr/ closure releases directly into the /dʒ/ articulation, often showing reduced or absent burst energy.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Syllabic /r̩/ with length before affricate; cold exclamation

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"Brr,"** slowly, establishing full /dr/ articulation.
2. Say **"George!"** starting with an isolated /dʒ/.
3. Merge: *Brr, George!* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/bɹ̩ː d͡ʒɔɹd͡ʒ/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /dr/ and /dʒ/.
* Alternate "dr-dʒ-dr-dʒ" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

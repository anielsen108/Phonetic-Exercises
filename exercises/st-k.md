# Coarticulation Study: /st/ + /k/

### Sequence: fricative + stop cluster → voiceless velar stop

### Example Phrase: **best car**

**IPA:** `/bɛst̚ kʰɑɹ/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[bɛst̚ kʰɑɹ[`

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the velar target before the /st/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and velar articulations.

2. **Stop Release Overlap:**
   The /st/ is typically unreleased ([st̚]), with air pressure maintained until the /k/ closure forms. The release burst belongs to /k/.

3. **Fricative-to-Stop Transition:**
   The continuous airflow of /st/ terminates as the /k/ closure forms. Listeners perceive a sharp acoustic boundary.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Cluster → velar stop

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"best"** slowly, establishing full /st/ articulation.
2. Say **"car"** starting with an isolated /k/.
3. Merge: *best car* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/bɛst̚ kʰɑɹ/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /st/ and /k/.
* Alternate "st-k-st-k" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

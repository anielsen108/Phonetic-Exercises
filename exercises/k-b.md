# Coarticulation Study: /k/ + /b/

### Sequence: voiceless velar stop → voiced bilabial stop

### Example Phrase: **back bone**

**IPA:** `/bæk̚ boʊ̯n/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[bæk̚ boʊ̯n[`

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the bilabial target before the /k/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between velar and bilabial articulations.

2. **Voicing Transition:**
   The glottis shifts from voiceless to voiced. This transition may show partial devoicing or voicing anticipation depending on speech rate.

3. **Stop Release Overlap:**
   The /k/ is typically unreleased ([k̚]), with air pressure maintained until the /b/ closure forms. The release burst belongs to /b/.

4. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Voiceless → voiced: /b/ may be partially devoiced

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"back"** slowly, establishing full /k/ articulation.
2. Say **"bone"** starting with an isolated /b/.
3. Merge: *back bone* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/bæk̚ boʊ̯n/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /k/ and /b/.
* Alternate "k-b-k-b" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.

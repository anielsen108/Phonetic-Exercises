# Coarticulation Study: /s/ + /f/

### Sequence: voiceless alveolar fricative → voiceless labiodental fricative

### Example Phrase: **this food**

**IPA:** `/ðɪs fud/`
**Allophonic detail:** `[ðɪs fud[`

---

## 1. Segmental Foundations

| Segment | Symbol | Place | Manner | Voicing | Airflow | Duration |
|----------|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| First | /s/ | alveolar | fricative (sibilant) | voiceless | oral | continuous, high-energy |
| Second | /f/ | labiodental | fricative | voiceless | oral | continuous, variable length |

* **/s/**: Alveolar → labiodental
* **/f/**: Voiceless labiodental fricative articulation follows with typical place and manner characteristics.

---

## 2. Coarticulatory Mechanisms

1. **Anticipatory Place Shift:**
   The tongue/lips begin moving toward the labiodental target before the /s/ gesture completes. This creates overlap between alveolar and labiodental articulations.

2. **Specific Adaptation:**
   Alveolar → labiodental

## 4. Articulatory Practice

### 4.1 Slow-Motion Drill

1. Say **"this"** slowly, establishing full /s/ articulation.
2. Say **"food"** starting with an isolated /f/.
3. Merge: *this food* — maintain voicing continuity, minimize release between segments.
4. Record and compare:

   * Over-released: Excessive separation between sounds
   * Balanced: Natural liaison as transcribed `/ðɪs fud/`
   * Under-articulated: Loss of primary articulatory cues

### 4.2 Gestural Continuity Exercise

* Focus awareness on active articulators for both /s/ and /f/.
* Alternate "s-f-s-f" while maintaining voicing where applicable.
* Notice the articulatory transition: where does one gesture end and the next begin?
* Perform slowly, then gradually increase speed while preserving clarity.
* The movement should feel **fluid** and **economical**, not **segmented**.
